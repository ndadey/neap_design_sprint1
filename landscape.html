The NAEP Output Landscape

Everything NAEP publishes — report cards, data tools, released items, microdata, and special studies — mapped to the six kinds of people who actually reach for it. Built to orient the sprint conversation about how stakeholders access, understand, and interact with NAEP data.

Snapshot: June 2026, current NAEP / Nation’s Report Card sites Sources: nces.ed.gov/nationsreportcard · nationsreportcard.gov Reads as: 1 map → 6 audience tables → 1 usage matrix

The whole landscape, in seven families

NAEP’s many products cluster into seven families. The detailed tables and the usage matrix below both use these same seven groupings.

Headline Report Cards

National results · "how are we doing"
  • The Nation’s Report Card (interactive results by subject)
  • Subjects: math, reading, science, writing, civics, U.S. history, geography, economics, TEL, arts
  • Grades 4, 8, 12 · achievement levels & trends
  • Long-Term Trend (ages 9 / 13 / 17, 50-yr)

State & District Results

Jurisdiction performance
  • NAEP State Results
  • State Profiles & District Profiles
  • Trial Urban District (TUDA) Results
  • State / District Snapshots (1-pagers)
  • Private school results

Data Explorer & API

Build-your-own tables
  • NAEP Data Explorer (NDE) — custom cross-tabs, significance, gap & regression analysis
  • Export to Word / Excel / PDF
  • NAEP Data Service API
  • Public/Private/Charter dashboard

Items & Frameworks

What’s tested · what scores mean
  • NAEP Questions Tool — 3,000+ released items, student responses, % correct
  • Item Maps & Achievement-Level Descriptions
  • Assessment Frameworks
  • Take NAEP Now · Sample-question booklets

Microdata & Methods

For secondary analysis
  • Restricted-Use Datasets (student-level)
  • EdSurvey & statistical software
  • Technical Documentation
  • Process Data (keystroke / timing)
  • Inclusion / accommodation data

Special Studies

Deep dives & linking
  • Achievement Gaps · Oral Reading Fluency
  • Mapping State Proficiency Standards + tool
  • Linking NAEP & TIMSS
  • High School Transcript Study · Nat’l Indian Education Study

Plain-Language & News

Explain & communicate
  • Newsroom & press releases
  • NAEP Plus Blog · Videos
  • Parent & student guides
  • Glossary · Focus on NAEP

How to read this for the sprint. Density falls off sharply toward the right and bottom: the products that are hardest to access (microdata, methods, special studies) reach the fewest audiences, while the same two or three headline products carry the load for everyone. Those concentration points — and the empty cells — are candidate territory for "where does access break down" and "what would a better NAEP surface to whom."
